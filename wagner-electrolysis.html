Resurrection by Electrolysis: A Wagner Ware Sidney‑O Skillet and Round Griddle

A $45 gamble at a Puyallup junk fair, a plastic tub, and some science.

April 2026  ·  TheToddMark

The Object

Two Wagner Ware Sidney‑O cast iron pieces — a #8 skillet and a #9 round griddle — heavily crusted, showing decades of baked-on carbon and rust. Not the kind of thing most people would look twice at. But the mark on the bottom — Wagner Ware, Sidney‑O — told me everything I needed to know.

Wagner Manufacturing opened in Sidney, Ohio in 1891. At its peak the company held a 60% share of the American cookware market. The "Sidney‑O" mark is the collector's tell: when the Wagner family sold the company in 1952 and production eventually shifted, that mark was phased out — disappearing from pieces after 1959. These pans predate that transition, which puts them solidly in the realm of genuine vintage American iron.

The Story

The Great Junk Hunt runs a couple of times a year at the Washington State Fairgrounds in Puyallup — hundreds of vendors, acres of stuff, and if you know what you're looking for, it's one of the better hunting grounds in the Pacific Northwest. Last Saturday was the April event.

I spotted these two sitting together, crusted over but intact — no cracks, no chips, handles solid. The bottom mark was legible through the grime. Forty-five dollars for both. That's the kind of math that makes sense when you know what's underneath.

Wagner's own advertising said it best: "We do not strive to manufacture hollow ware as cheaply as possible, but as good as it can be made." That ethic is still in the iron, nearly a century later.

The question was never whether these were worth restoring. It was just which method. With this level of buildup — thick carbon, active rust — electrolysis was the right starting point. It works slowly and gently, without chemicals that can etch or damage the iron. Spoiler: it wouldn't be the only method needed.

The Process

What is electrolysis?

Electrolysis uses low-voltage DC current to loosen and lift baked-on carbon, and to convert iron oxide (rust) into magnetite — a stable black iron compound that scrubs off cleanly. The pan becomes the cathode (negative terminal); sacrificial steel anodes — in this case, sections of rebar — are the positive terminal. The electrolyte is a mild solution of washing soda (sodium carbonate) in water. Current flows, bubbles form on the pan surface, and rust and crud release into the solution.

It's slow, safe, and remarkably effective on cast iron that would be risky to clean aggressively by other means. The iron itself is not harmed — you're only removing what doesn't belong there.

Safety first Electrolysis carries very low risk of shock, but low isn't zero. Don't touch the water, the pans, or the wires until you are 100% certain the power is off — and I mean unplug the power supply, not just switch it off. Better safe than sorry.

More importantly: do this outside or in a well-ventilated space, and keep it away from any open flame or spark source. Electrolysis splits water into its components — pure hydrogen and pure oxygen. The quantities produced are small, but in a confined space they can accumulate. Hydrogen is extremely flammable. Ignite that mixture and you've got your own personal, mini-Hindenburg situation. Outside on the deck is the right call.

The Outcome

The two pieces were run through the tank separately. The #9 griddle went first — about 8 hours, set up and pulled the same day. The #8 skillet was the harder case: it went in Sunday evening and ran nearly 24 hours, pulled Monday morning. Coming back to a tank of near-black water and four rebar anodes buried in orange foam is a satisfying sight. It means the process was productive the entire time.

When electrolysis isn't enough — Easy-Off to the rescue

Here's the honest part. Electrolysis improved both pans dramatically. But after pulling them from the tank, scrubbing with steel wool, working at stubborn spots with a putty knife, and trying Comet — there was still more baked-on carbon than was acceptable, particularly on the skillet. Nearly 24 hours of electrochemistry and manual effort, and it still wasn't there. Decades of polymerized seasoning and carbonized grease don't always yield to one method alone.

The solution: Easy-Off Heavy Duty Oven Cleaner (yellow cap) on both pans. Each went into a sealed garbage bag with a heavy coat, left to work. The active ingredient is sodium hydroxide — lye — which saponifies the remaining carbon and fat residue, converting it into something that wipes and scrubs away. Same chemistry as a lye bath, just in aerosol form and far more accessible.

The real restoration sequence Electrolysis handles rust and loosens carbon. Easy-Off handles the remaining polymerized carbon that electrolysis lifted but couldn't fully remove. These aren't competing methods — they're complementary. Use whichever the pan needs, or both.

The scrub-down

Post-Easy-Off, both pans got a thorough scrub with 000 steel wool, then a full wash with a soft nylon brush and Dawn dish soap. Yes — soap on cast iron, and that's exactly right at this stage. You're starting from bare metal; soap won't hurt bare iron. The goal here is clean.

Drying

Once satisfied with the bare metal, both pans went into the oven at 200°F for 20 minutes. Cast iron is porous, and any moisture hiding in those pores will cause flash rust the moment seasoning oil goes on. Low heat drives it all out. The pans come out warm, fully dry, and slightly grey — ready to accept oil.

Seasoning — and a confession

The plan was avocado oil: neutral flavor, high smoke point (~500°F), polymerizes well at seasoning temperatures. What actually got grabbed for the first three coats was grapeseed oil. Wrong bottle.

Grapeseed isn't a disaster — it has a reasonable smoke point and does polymerize — but avocado was the intention, and things were looking pretty good regardless. After catching the mistake, the cooking surfaces on both pans got a light buff with 0000 steel wool to knock down any high spots from the first three coats, followed by a thorough wipe-down to remove every trace of steel wool fragments before the next coat went on. Then three more sessions with the right oil.

Six total seasoning sessions. Three grapeseed, three avocado. The pans don't know the difference — they just look black and almost shiny.

The wrong bottle. La Tourangelle Grapeseed Oil — not what was planned, not a disaster either.

The technique that actually works

The seasoning technique Apply oil over the entire pan — inside, outside, handle, everywhere. Then wipe nearly all of it off. Apply it like it was a mistake. The layer left behind should be almost invisible; if the pan looks oily, it's too much. Place the pan upside down in a 450°F convection oven and bake for one hour. Let it cool in the oven until handleable, then repeat. Multiple thin coats beat one thick coat every time. Thick oil causes gumminess and flaking; thin oil polymerizes into a hard, slick surface.
