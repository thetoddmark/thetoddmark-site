Restoration No. 06

Imperial Steak Knife
Handle Refresh

A set of three becomes a set of six — CA glue, 220 grit, and three coats of Walrus Oil

01 / What I'm Working With

Circa 1990s–2000s. Mass-market. Stamped "STAINLESS CHINA." Somehow always in the drawer.

These knives aren't fancy. The blades are stamped IMPERIAL and STAINLESS CHINA — about nine inches overall, with a fully serrated four-and-a-half to five inch blade. The handles appear to be beech or rubberwood, secured with two large brass compression rivets. Google tells me they were sold in boxed sets of six to twelve, often destined for restaurant supply closets or a spot in the back of a kitchen drawer.

I had three. I genuinely don't know where we got them — they'd just always been there, in the knife drawer, doing their job quietly for an unknowable number of years. The wood handles were clearly overdue for some attention, and I needed a low-stakes project with a decent chance of a satisfying result. I found a matching set of four on eBay for $10 — enough to bring the total to six with one to spare.

"The kind of knives you reach for when you've had a week that screams 'I deserve a steak tonight' — not the ones you bring out for company."

02 / As Found

Bone dry. Chalky. Dishwasher survivors.

Two words covered it: bone dry. These knives had been run through the dishwasher countless times, left submerged in soapy water, and generally subjected to the kind of treatment that wood handles were never designed to tolerate. The grain was washed out and almost chalky in appearance. The open grain was clearly visible, and the ends of the handles had some rough shaping and small checks — cracks running with the grain direction. The brass rivets were dull and dark. On a couple of them, the blade had worked slightly loose in the handle.

03 / The Plan

Stabilize first, sand second, oil third.

Before doing anything else, I needed to address the structural issues — the loose blades and the handle checks. Thin CA glue was the right tool for both: it wicks into gaps by capillary action, locks things in place, and dries clear and hard.

From there, the plan was straightforward: sand through 80, 120, 180, and 220 grit to open the grain and smooth the handles; wipe down with mineral spirits; then apply three coats of Walrus Oil (polymerized tung oil) with 24 hours of cure time between each coat and a light buff of 0000 steel wool between coats. Finish with Renaissance Wax.

  • 01 Thin CA glue for loose blades and handle checks
  • 02 Sand 80 → 120 → 180 → 220 grit, mineral spirits wipe-down
  • 03 Three coats of Walrus Oil, 24-hour cure between each
  • 04 Renaissance Wax for protection and feel
04 / Handle Repairs

Thin CA glue and clamps. Ten minutes of work, two hours of patience.

The loose blades and handle checks were addressed with thin CA glue — the watery kind that wicks into gaps before you even realize it's moving. For the handle cracks, I applied glue along the length of each check, let it wick in, then clamped while the accelerator did its job. For the loose blades, I worked a small amount of glue into the gap between blade and handle and held firm pressure for a few minutes.

05 / Sanding

80 grit to open the grain. 220 grit to close it back up.

I'll be honest — I sanded most of these handles on a phone call with a buddy and didn't stop to document every stage. Not my finest journalistic moment. But the process was the same as always: 80 grit first to remove the worst of the surface dullness and open the grain, then 120 and 180 to refine, and finally 220 to smooth everything out before oiling.

What surprised me was how much grain was hiding under all that damage. As the pale, chalky surface came off, the wood underneath started to show real character — tight ring patterns, some mild figure, warm tones that hadn't been visible in years. That's always the moment in a project like this when you stop feeling skeptical about whether it was worth the effort.

After finishing through 220, I wiped each handle down with mineral spirits and a clean rag to remove all remaining dust and residue. Then I let them sit for several hours until completely dry. Patience at this stage pays off — oiling on top of any remaining solvent or moisture leads to an uneven, blotchy first coat.

06 / Three Coats of Walrus Oil

Apply light. Wipe off everything you can. Wait 24 hours. Repeat.

Each coat of Walrus Oil goes on the same way: dip a fingertip, rub the handle until fully covered — avoiding heavy pooling — then set a 15-minute timer. When it goes off, wipe every bit of excess off with a blue shop towel or lint-free rag. If you leave too much oil on the surface it will either take forever to cure or develop a gummy, sticky texture that no one wants to touch. Less is more.

The second coat, after a 24-hour wait and a light buff with 0000 steel wool, started to even out the splotchiness that often lingers after the first application. The tone was normalizing — handles that looked slightly inconsistent were beginning to read as a set.

The third coat — another steel wool buff beforehand, another 24-hour wait after — left the handles looking genuinely warm and rich. The kind of result that makes you remember why this is worth doing.

One note on curing conditions: room temperature around 68–72°F with reasonable airflow is ideal. Avoid the temptation to speed things up with a heat source — elevated temperatures can cure the surface layers quickly while leaving the oil underneath still soft, which produces a finish that feels wrong underhand and doesn't hold up.

07 / The Complication

The new eBay set arrived. And suddenly the original three didn't look finished.

The four knives I'd ordered to complete the set arrived, and they were clearly the same model — very similar shape and hardware. The issue was that new and shiny next to just refinished made the three originals look like they hadn't quite gotten there. Even after careful work, there was enough of a visual gap that I wanted to close it.

So I did the logical thing: I used 220 grit to gently knock back the factory finish on the new knives and polish up the brass rivets, then hit the three originals again with 180 grit to reopen the grain slightly. Then all seven went back through a fresh coat of Walrus Oil — same process, one coat, 24-hour cure — so the whole batch would come out of finishing together.

It was the right call. When they all dried, the difference between old and new was essentially gone.

08 / The Result

Six matching knives. One spare. Back in the drawer where they belong.

09 / The Verdict

A few hours of actual work, a lot of patience, and $10.

The Honest Take

These aren't museum pieces and they never will be. The blades are serviceable stainless, the construction is simple, and the whole set cost less than a mid-range restaurant entrée. But that's exactly why this project was satisfying — it proved that even the most ordinary kitchen tool is worth taking care of when the materials underneath the neglect are still perfectly good.

The wood was always in there. It just needed someone to bother. The blades are now tight, the rivets shine, the grain is visible and warm, and the handles feel genuinely good in the hand. Will they impress dinner guests? Probably not. But they'll do their job well, and they'll look the part while doing it. Sometimes that's enough.

Mistakes made, lesson learned: In my eagerness to get started, I began sanding the wood scales while on the phone with a good friend. I failed to tape off the steel where it meets the handle and — given my lack of attention (it was a GOOD phone call) — I ended up scratching the steel a bit while sanding. Next time: slow down, think it through.

10 / Supplies Used

Everything that touched these knives.

Some links below are affiliate links — I earn a small commission if you buy through them, at no extra cost to you. I only list what I actually used.

  • Thin CA Glue Wicks into handle checks and blade gaps by capillary action. Dries clear and hard. Used with an accelerator for faster cure.
  • Mineral Spirits Post-sanding wipe-down to clear dust and residue before oiling. Also good for confirming the wood is clean and dry.
  • Sandpaper (80, 120, 180, 220 grit) 80 to open the grain and remove surface damage, 120–180 to refine, 220 to smooth before oiling. I use 3M Cubitron — it cuts cleaner and lasts longer.
  • Walrus Oil (Polymerized Tung Oil) Three coats, 24 hours between each, 0000 steel wool buff before coats two and three. Applied thin, wiped off thoroughly. Food-safe once cured.
  • 0000 Steel Wool Light buffing between oil coats to knock back any raised grain or dust nibs. Very light touch — you're not sanding, just smoothing.
  • Blue Shop Towels For wiping excess oil off the handles. Lint-free and disposable — critical when you need to remove every bit of surface oil.
  • Renaissance Wax Final coat — applied thin, hazed 5–10 minutes, buffed off. Adds protection and a subtle sheen without looking artificial.
