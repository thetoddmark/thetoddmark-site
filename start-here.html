Start Here — Vintage Tool Restoration | The Todd·Mark

I restore old tools. Not because they’re antiques — I frequently find old tools to be genuinely better than most of what’s made today, and often far more interesting, useful, and frankly, beautiful and charming.

I’m not a professional restorer. I work in healthcare technology by day, and increasingly spend my evenings arguing with myself on eBay about whether a rusty screwdriver, hand plane, Yankee driver, or antique smudge pot counts as a good deal. (It often does.) I have a tiny workshop (hoping to upgrade that soon — see the workshop project), some sandpaper, a bottle of Fiebing’s leather dye, and a growing conviction that old tools deserve better than a landfill. Eventually I plan to resell some of my restorations to fund the next find, but for now I’m treating them like the One Ring: My Precious.

Every restoration on this site is documented in full — where the tool came from, what it cost, what it needed, and what it looks like when it’s ready to work again. The techniques that worked. The ones that didn’t. The occasional tool that turns out to be worth far more time than it cost.

How I do this

Every restoration starts with research. Know what you have — find out what’s missing, uncover the history of the object as best you can. Google helps. AI helps more than you’d expect. I identified and dated a Stanley Bailey No. 5 — full type study, approximate year, what to look for — from a few photos I snapped at a garage sale while the seller was watching me. Would have taken an hour of forum-diving later. Took five minutes standing in the driveway. You still have to verify what you find, but those tools can genuinely accelerate the most important question: “Great, I have a 1908 X — what do I do next?”

Very often, the answer is: clean. Clean what’s there, and don’t remove more than you have to. Whether you’re dealing with wooden handles or metal bits and bobs — once you’ve brought the item back into its former shape, finish in a way that protects the material without hiding it. For wood, that almost always means a penetrating oil — I’ve had great results with Walrus Oil (polymerized tung oil), applied thin, wiped off before it gets tacky, two or three coats. For metal, work with the patina rather than against it. And almost always, I finish with a coat or two of Renaissance Wax — it protects against oxidation, brings out the beauty of the material, and leaves a hand feel that makes the tool an actual pleasure to pick up.
