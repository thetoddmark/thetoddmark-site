Bridgeport H.M. Corp
Interchangeable Tool Handle

01 / What I'm Working With

Bridgeport, Connecticut. Clever design. Eight tools in one handle.

This tool was manufactured by Bridgeport H.M. Corp. of Bridgeport, Connecticut. The only marking visible on the tool itself appears on the knurled steel chuck and reads BRIDGEPORT H.M. CORP — beyond that stamp, there are no model numbers or patent markings visible.

The design is straightforward but clever. The tool consists of a turned hardwood handle — rosewood, I believe — that unscrews to reveal a hollow storage cavity. Inside that cavity are eight interchangeable tool bits. At the front of the handle is a steel chuck that uses a four-jaw spring collet to grip whichever bit is inserted. Tightening the knurled collar compresses the jaws and locks the tool bit in place. It's a simple mechanism, but it works surprisingly well.

The bits included with this example appear to be: three slotted screwdrivers, an awl, a punch, a gimlet (screw starter), a small saw blade, and a curved gouge.

Tools like this were intended to provide a compact collection of commonly needed hand tools in a single handle — useful in small toolboxes, slipped into a tool belt or pocket, or on workbenches where space was limited.

02 / Before

Honest working condition. Decades of use. Good bones.

When the tool arrived it was in what I would describe as honest working condition. Nothing was broken, missing, or obviously abused — which is always a good sign. But decades of use had left their mark. The hardwood handle had accumulated a layer of grime and oils from years of handling, making it difficult to be confident about which wood species I was dealing with. The steel components showed light oxidation and dullness. The bits themselves had small areas of corrosion and wear from previous use.

The chuck still functioned properly and the threads in the handle were intact, but the tool definitely needed a thorough cleaning. Fortunately, projects like this are usually more about careful cleaning and preservation than heavy restoration.

03 / The Plan

Restraint, not reinvention.

With tools like this, restraint is usually a wise approach. My goal was not to make the tool look brand new. Instead, the objective was to:

  • 01 Remove dirt and oxidation
  • 02 Stabilize the metal components
  • 03 Refresh the wood handle
  • 04 Preserve the original character of the tool

No aggressive sanding. No heavy polishing. No changes to the original design. Just careful cleaning and a light touch.

04 / Cleaning the Steel Components

Mineral spirits, a nylon brush, and patience.

The steel parts — the chuck and all eight tool bits — were cleaned using mineral spirits and a nylon brush to remove accumulated dirt and oils. Light surface oxidation was addressed with a fine Scotch-Brite pad. The goal here was simply to remove active corrosion while preserving the original surface finish.

Once cleaned, the metal parts were wiped down and given a very light coat of oil to help prevent future rust. The chuck mechanism itself was flushed with solvent and gently brushed to remove debris from inside the collet jaws. After cleaning, the chuck operated smoothly again and gripped the bits securely.

Finally, on the metal parts I deployed Flitz to clean and provide a bit of polish without overdoing it.

05 / Cleaning the Handle

The rosewood gamble pays off.

I have a bit of a thing for Brazilian Rosewood handles on my tools. When I saw this on eBay I took a leap of faith that the description was accurate and that it was indeed rosewood — it was pretty hard to tell for sure from the photos. And when it arrived, it was still pretty hard to tell just by looking at it.

The cleaning process begins to reveal what we're working with. The hardwood handle was cleaned with mineral spirits to remove surface grime and old oils. This step alone made a significant difference — what had previously looked like a dull, almost black plastic surface gradually revealed hints of a deep reddish-brown tone and visible grain.

"I wasn't trying to bring this back to a 'brand-new' appearance — but I did want to make it beautiful."

Sanding grits 80 → 120 → 180 → 220 were deployed to even out some of the dents and bring out the beauty of the wood.

After sanding, I wiped it down a couple of times with mineral spirits and a clean rag — repeating until wiping the wood began to leave less and less residue on the rags. At that point, patience was required. I let it dry completely overnight.

In the morning, the wood was fully dry and it was time for the beauty treatments. For this tool I applied several coats of boiled linseed oil (BLO). The idea was to give the wood some much-needed oil into the fibers and reveal the grain. I applied the first coat and let it sit for about 10 minutes before wiping off every little bit I could. The first coat dried for about 8 hours before the second was applied — which I then let dry overnight.

Other responsibilities prevented me from touching the project for about 24 hours after the second BLO coat and wipe-down. Eventually that third BLO application happened, which I once again wiped as dry as possible about 10 minutes after application and then let the handles dry and the BLO cure for a full 48 hours. It felt like an eternity.

When the time finally arrived I did a gentle buff/scuff with 0000 steel wool, wiped it completely clean with a lint-free cloth, and then applied 2 coats of shellac — allowing about an hour between coats and buffing with the steel wool again after each was completely dry. Last but not least, I applied renaissance wax very thinly, letting it haze over (5–10 minutes) before buffing with another clean lint-free cloth.

In my opinion, the result was a finish that looks natural and appropriate for the age of the tool without appearing overly glossy or artificial. It also has good hand feel — it isn't sticky at all, but the combination used in refinishing (especially the wax) helps me secure it confidently when gripping and using the tool.

06 / Reassembly

Everything fit together exactly as it should.

Once all of the components were cleaned and dry, the tool was reassembled. The bits were returned to their storage compartment inside the handle, and the threaded cap was screwed back into place. The chuck was lightly lubricated and tested with each bit to confirm proper operation.

07 / The Result

Much closer to what it looked like leaving the factory.

After cleaning and reassembly, the tool looks much closer to what it likely did when it left the factory many decades ago. The steel components now show their machining and knurling clearly. The stamped BRIDGEPORT H.M. CORP marking is crisp and easy to read. The handle has regained its warm color and grain. More importantly, the tool functions exactly as intended. Each bit locks securely in the chuck, and the entire set stores neatly inside the handle.

It's a simple design, but a thoughtful one.

08 / The Verdict

$34.95. Very satisfying little restoration.

The Honest Take

Tools like this represent a fascinating moment in tool design history. Long before plastic seemed to take over the world and before modern interchangeable driver bits and multi-tools became common, toolmakers were already experimenting with ways to pack multiple functions into a single compact tool.

This Bridgeport H.M. Corp. interchangeable handle is one example of that idea. Is it perfect? Not exactly — the chuck isn't as precise as modern collets, and swapping bits takes a bit more effort than modern quick-change systems. But it works. And more importantly, it shows how toolmakers were thinking about versatility and efficiency long before those ideas became marketing buzzwords.

One honest note: while I love how this turned out, if I could go back and start over, I'd forego the BLO and use Walrus Oil (polymerized tung oil) instead. C'est la vie.

09 / Supplies Used

Everything that touched this tool.

Some links below are affiliate links — I earn a small commission if you buy through them, at no extra cost to you. I only list what I actually used.

  • Flitz Metal Polish For the steel parts — cleans and polishes without overdoing it. Light touch, good results.
  • Mineral Spirits Initial wipe-down on both wood and metal to cut through accumulated grime and oils before any other treatment.
  • Nylon Brushes For scrubbing the steel parts with mineral spirits — soft enough to not scratch, stiff enough to get into the knurling.
  • Scotch-Brite Pads (Fine) For light surface oxidation on the steel — removes corrosion without altering the original surface finish.
  • Walrus Oil (Polymerized Tung Oil) What I'd use if doing this again — polymerized tung oil with a cleaner cure than BLO. See Final Thoughts above.
  • Renaissance Wax Final coat on the wood. Applied thin, hazed for 5–10 minutes, buffed with a clean lint-free cloth. Museum-grade wax that protects without changing the look.
  • Light Machine Oil (3-In-One) For lubricating the chuck collet mechanism after cleaning. A small amount goes a long way.
